<div id="what-is-intent-data">
    <h2 className="text-2xl font-bold mt-12 mb-4">1) What Is Intent Data?</h2>

    <p>Intent data is information that reveals when a company or person is actively researching a purchase. It gets captured through website visits, content downloads, search behavior, and social engagement.</p>

    <p>Nobody buys B2B software cold. They research first. According to Google research, B2B buyers run an average of 12 online searches before they ever visit a brand's website. Along the way they read comparison articles, download the occasional whitepaper, and comment on LinkedIn posts about the exact problem they're trying to solve.</p>

    <p>Every one of those actions leaves a digital breadcrumb, and intent data is what reads the trail. It's the difference between guessing and knowing that a particular company is shopping for something like what you sell.</p>

    <p>The reason this is worth caring about comes down to timing. Reach out to someone who's already deep in research and you read as helpful. Cold-blast someone who has never once thought about your product and you read as spam. Intent data is mostly a tool for staying on the helpful side of that line.</p>
</div>

<div id="types-of-intent-data">
    <h2 className="text-2xl font-bold mt-12 mb-4">2) Types of Intent Data</h2>

    <p>Not all intent data is the same, and before you compare a single tool you need the three main categories straight. This is where most guides drown you in jargon, so I'll keep it plain. I've read enough whitepapers to know which distinctions actually change a buying decision and which ones are just filler.</p>

    <h3 className="text-xl font-semibold mt-8 mb-3">First-Party Intent Data</h3>

    <p>First-party intent data is the set of signals collected from properties you own: website visits, email opens, content downloads, and social engagement on posts you can access. Add chatbot conversations and email clicks to that list too.</p>

    <p>What most teams miss is that LinkedIn engagement counts here as well. When someone comments on a competitor's post about a problem you solve, that's a first-party signal you can capture directly, and it's a strong one.</p>

    <p>According to Foundry research, companies that prioritize first-party intent data are 2.3x more likely to outperform revenue goals.</p>

    <p>Tools like Guffles pull these engagement signals straight out of LinkedIn posts, which hands small teams first-party intent without building any tracking infrastructure of their own. Someone engages with content in your industry, you catch the signal, and you reach out while the context is still fresh. This way of <Link href="/blog/find-warm-leads-social-engagement-signals">finding warm leads through social engagement</Link> gets overlooked a lot, mostly because it doesn't fit the tidy "third-party data provider" box people expect.</p>

    <p>The upside is accuracy. You're capturing real engagement, not modeled behavior. The catch is reach: you only see people who are already active in your space.</p>

    <h3 className="text-xl font-semibold mt-8 mb-3">Second-Party Intent Data</h3>

    <p>Second-party intent data comes from trusted partner platforms like G2, TrustRadius, and Capterra. People call these "downstream intent" signals because they catch buyers in the act of comparing products and pricing, which is about as far down the funnel as a signal gets.</p>

    <p>Downstream intent, then, is second-party data from review platforms like G2 and TrustRadius, and it captures buyers who are actively weighing one product against another.</p>

    <p>When someone lands on your G2 profile or reads reviews of your competitors, that's high-intent behavior worth acting on. The catch is the price: G2 Buyer Intent starts around $40K-$50K/year, which is not what I'd call small-team friendly.</p>

    <h3 className="text-xl font-semibold mt-8 mb-3">Third-Party Intent Data</h3>

    <p>Third-party intent data is collected from external websites and platforms, then aggregated by providers like Bombora that track behavior across thousands of B2B sites.</p>

    <p>Bombora alone tracks 5,000+ B2B websites. When a company shows "surge" activity on topics tied to your product, you get an alert. The reach is enormous, but you pay for that scale, and the accuracy can wobble because you're looking at modeled behavior rather than direct engagement.</p>

Gartner found that technology providers using third-party intent data are 2.09x more likely to hit 10%+ conversion rates. The <Link href="/blog/warm-vs-cold-outreach-data-breakdown">warm vs cold outreach data</Link> says the same thing at scale: warm leads from engagement signals close at 14.6% against 1.7% for cold outreach, an 8.5x gap.</p>

<div id="providers-by-tier">
    <h2 className="text-2xl font-bold mt-12 mb-4">6) Intent Data Providers by Budget Tier</h2>

    <p>This is the comparison you came for. Every tool with real pricing attached, none of the "contact sales" runaround.</p>

    <h3 className="text-xl font-semibold mt-8 mb-3">Enterprise Tier ($15K+/year)</h3>

    <p>I'll be blunt: if your team is under 50 people, skip this tier. It's still worth a quick read so you know what the high end actually offers.</p>

    <p><strong>Bombora</strong>: $30K-$100K+/year. Tracks 5,000+ B2B websites for topic-based intent signals. It's the gold standard for third-party intent, but you'll need a dedicated ops person to implement it properly.</p>

    <p><strong>6sense</strong>: $60K-$300K/year. AI-driven predictions paired with ABM orchestration. The implementation is involved and the contracts are long, which makes sense given it's built for mature marketing organizations with real headcount.</p>

    <p><strong>Demandbase</strong>: $18K-$100K+/year. An ABM platform that folds intent in with advertising. Enterprise marketing teams tend to love it.</p>

    <p><strong>ZoomInfo</strong>: $15K-$40K+/year. 210M+ contacts, with intent data showing up in the higher tiers. Those cold emails that cite "ZoomInfo data"? This is where they come from.</p>

    <p><strong>My take</strong>: powerful tools, but overkill for a small team. The setup alone demands resources most startups simply don't have lying around.</p>

    <h3 className="text-xl font-semibold mt-8 mb-3">Mid-Market Tier ($1K-5K/year)</h3>

    <p>This is where it starts feeling realistic for a growing team.</p>

    <p><strong>Apollo.io</strong>: $49-119/user/month (annual). Buying intent comes baked in with 6-12 topics depending on your plan, alongside email sequences and CRM integration. It holds up well for SDR teams of 5-20 people, and even the free tier carries some limited intent data.</p>

    <p><strong>Cognism</strong>: $25K+/year, custom pricing. A Bombora intent partnership layered on phone-verified data, and it's genuinely strong in EMEA if you sell internationally. The price puts it at the top end of mid-market.</p>

    <p><strong>Lead411</strong>: $75-99/user/month. Pairs Bombora intent data with unlimited email views and growth signals. Good value for SMBs that want traditional third-party intent without paying enterprise rates for it.</p>

    <p><strong>My take</strong>: Apollo is the one I'd point most small teams toward. The per-user pricing scales sanely, and the intent topics are in the base plans rather than locked behind an upsell.</p>

    <h3 className="text-xl font-semibold mt-8 mb-3">Accessible Tier (Under $1K/year)</h3>

    <p>This is where small teams should actually start, proving out the ROI of intent data before they commit to anything bigger.</p>

    <p><strong>Guffles</strong>: $79/month with $150 in wallet credits. It pulls engagement signals straight from LinkedIn posts, so when someone likes or comments on a competitor's post, Guffles captures that signal and lets you filter it against your ICP. That's first-party intent data with no enterprise price tag and no per-user fees, and it fits best for teams that treat LinkedIn as a real lead source.</p>

    <p><strong>Lusha</strong>: $22-70/user/month. Contact enrichment, with intent data showing up in the premium tiers. It works for individual contributors who mainly need contact info and a bit of intent context on the side. The cheaper tiers lean far more toward contact data than intent.</p>

    <p><strong>Dealfront/Leadfeeder</strong>: $99-165/month. Website visitor identification, so you can see which companies are browsing your site. Best for inbound-focused teams that already have meaningful traffic.</p>

    <p><strong>My take</strong>: for a team under 10 people spending under $200/month, Guffles does something the others don't. Lusha and Dealfront are built around contact enrichment and website visitors; Guffles is built around social engagement intent. Same rough price, different bet. Pick the one that matches how you already prospect.</p>

In my experience a lot of small teams find the first-party signals are plenty. You rarely need to know every website a prospect visited; knowing they commented on a competitor's post is usually the signal that matters.</p>

    <p><strong>Step 3: Check CRM integrations.</strong> Confirm the tool actually connects to your stack, whether that's Salesforce, HubSpot, or Pipedrive. Integration complexity is all over the map.
