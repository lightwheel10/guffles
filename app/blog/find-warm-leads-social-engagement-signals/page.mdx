<div id="section-2">
    <SectionTitle title="2) Why Social Engagement Signals Reveal Buyer Intent">
        Social engagement signals are just the actions people take on social platforms: likes, comments, shares, questions. Every one of them tells you a little about what that person cares about and what they're dealing with.

        This matters because [75% of B2B buyers use social media to support their buying decisions](https://business.linkedin.com/sales-solutions/social-selling), and for C-level execs it's 84%. They're not passively scrolling; they're engaging with content about the exact problems they're trying to solve.

        A few examples. Someone commenting on a post about CRM implementation headaches probably has CRM implementation headaches. Someone liking your competitor's post is likely in research mode for something like what you sell. Someone sharing an article about sales-team problems is clearly chewing on those problems.

        Buyer intent signals are the observable actions that show someone is actively researching or weighing a purchase. And [97% of B2B companies now say intent data gives them a competitive edge](https://www.theinsightcollective.com/insights/b2b-intent-data-statistics).

        You can track all this by hand, setting up LinkedIn notifications and checking engagement every day, or you can let a tool do it. Something like [Guffles](https://guffles.com) watches posts across your industry and pulls everyone who engaged. Manual or automated, the principle holds: engagement is intent.

<div id="section-3">
    <SectionTitle title="3) 5 Engagement Signals That Help You Find Warm Leads on LinkedIn">
        Here are the five signals I actually look for when I'm hunting warm leads on LinkedIn.

        **Signal 1: Comments on Pain-Point Posts**
        This is the strongest one. If someone bothers to comment on a post about a specific problem, they almost certainly have that problem. Find posts about the challenges you solve and actually read the comments. When you see "we struggle with this too" or "this is exactly what my team deals with," that's a warm lead.

        **Signal 2: Likes on Competitor Content**
        Anyone liking your competitors' content is in the market in some way: a current customer eyeing alternatives, a prospect comparing options, or someone keeping tabs on the space. All of them beat a random stranger.

        **Signal 3: Shares of Industry Insights**
        Sharing an industry report or a thought-leadership piece is a step up from a like. They cared enough to attach their name to it, and the people who do that tend to be the decision-makers or internal influencers you want.

        **Signal 4: Questions Asked in Groups or Discussions**
        When someone asks "what tools do you use for X?" or "how does your team handle Y?", they're openly shopping. Group threads and busy comment sections are full of these, and most people scroll right past them.

        **Signal 5: Engagement with Hiring or Growth Posts**
        Hiring is a buying signal in disguise. A company announcing five new SDR hires probably needs tooling to support them. Watch engagement on hiring posts, growth milestones, and team-expansion announcements.
    </SectionTitle>

<div id="section-4">
    <SectionTitle title="4) How to Track Engagement Signals to Find Warm Leads">
        Knowing which signals to watch is one thing; tracking them without losing your week is another. Here's the manual version and how to scale past it.

        **The Manual Approach**

        You can start today with zero tools. The routine:

        **Step 1:** Pick 10-15 relevant posts a week, from influencers, competitors, and industry voices talking about what your prospects care about.

        **Step 2:** Open the engagement on each one. See who liked, commented, and shared, then glance at their profiles. Do they fit your ICP?

        **Step 3:** Drop the ones who fit into a spreadsheet, with the post they engaged with, how they engaged, and a few profile details.

        **Step 4:** Use that context when you message them. Name the specific post and topic.

        It works, but there's a ceiling. By hand you can realistically cover 5-10 posts a week. Past that, it quietly becomes a full-time job.

        **Scaling with Intent-Based Prospecting Tools**

        The problem with the manual version is obvious: it doesn't scale. To watch hundreds of posts, you need automation.

        Intent-based lead discovery just means finding prospects from their behavior instead of static demographic data. [Guffles](https://guffles.com), for instance, takes any LinkedIn post URL, pulls everyone who engaged, and scores each one on how well they match your ICP.

        At $79/month against $300+ for the likes of Apollo or ZoomInfo, it's within reach for a smaller team, and it works off what people actually did rather than a static database.
    </SectionTitle>

<div id="section-5">
    <SectionTitle title="5) How to Reach Out to Warm Leads Without Sounding Generic">
        Finding warm leads is half of it. You still have to message them in a way that earns a reply, and the nice thing is warm leads hand you the context for free.

        The structure I use:

        **Reference the engagement first.** Open with the specific post or content they engaged with. That alone separates you from every cold message in their inbox.

        **Lead with the topic, not your product.** If they commented on a post about pipeline problems, talk about pipeline problems. Don't lunge for the pitch.

        **Ask one real question.** You want a reply, not a monologue, so ask about their experience with the problem or what they've already tried.

        **Keep it short.** Three or four sentences. Warm leads reply more often, but only when replying is easy.

        Here's an example:

        "Hi Sarah, I saw your comment on John's post about the challenges of tracking prospect engagement. It resonated because I hear that frustration a lot from sales teams. What have you tried so far to get better visibility into who's actually interested? Would love to compare notes."

        Notice what isn't there: no pitch, no product name, no "book a demo" link. With warm outreach you're trying to start a conversation, not close on the first touch.
    </SectionTitle>
