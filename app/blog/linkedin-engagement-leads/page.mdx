<div id="section-2">
  <SectionTitle title="What LinkedIn Engagement Signals Actually Tell You">
    Not every bit of engagement means the same thing, and it took me embarrassingly long to stop treating them as equal.

    A like is the cheapest signal there is. Somebody tapped a button. It tells you they didn't hate the post and not much more, but you get a flood of them, so they're useful in bulk. A comment is a different animal. Writing one costs effort, you have to read the thing, have a take, and put your name on it in public, so when someone writes "we've been fighting this exact problem all quarter" under a post about cold outreach, they've basically handed you a reason to reach out. Reposts are rarer and louder again. Sharing something is a small public endorsement; those people don't just agree, they want their own network to see it.

    So my rough order is commenters first, then likers when I need the numbers. How hard I lean on each comes down to how much outreach I can actually do that week. Twenty messages a day and I'm only touching commenters. Trying to scale it up and the likers start earning their keep.

    There's a nice side effect to all of this too. [Posts with more than five comments get 2.5x the views](https://ligo.ertiqah.com/blog/why-linkedin-comments-matter-more-than-posts-and-how-to-leverage-them-in-2025), and ones with comments are 8x more likely to get shared, so the high-engagement posts are exactly the ones surfacing the most prospects. <div id="section-4">
  <SectionTitle title="How to Find Warm Leads From LinkedIn Post Engagement">
    Here's the process I actually run, give or take. It's the same shape whether you do it by hand or pay a tool to do it for you, and it's the residue of a few hundred campaigns, plenty of them bad.

    ### Step 1: Identify High-Value Posts to Mine

    Most posts aren't worth mining. I go after three kinds. Competitor posts are the best, because anyone arguing in the comments under your competitor's content is shopping for what you sell whether they'd admit it or not. Posts from the loud voices in your niche are good too, since their audience self-selected for caring about your problem. And if you publish anything yourself, your own commenters are the warmest of the bunch, they already know who you are. One filter I never skip: ignore anything under fifty engagements. Below that there just isn't enough to work with.

    ### Step 2: Extract the Engagers

    Three ways to get them out, roughly in order of pain. By hand is free, miserable, and fine for a one-off. A browser extension is the middle road, faster than hand, cheaper than a real tool. A dedicated tool is the fastest and the only thing that holds up once you're doing this every week. If you want to prove the idea before spending anything, start by hand, there's no shame in it. Just don't move in.

    ### Step 3: Filter by ICP Criteria

    This is the step people skip and the one that decides whether any of it works. A viral post might pull a couple thousand likes, and maybe forty of those people are actually your buyer. So before you message anyone, cut the list down to people who genuinely fit, the right job title, a company size you sell to, an industry you can serve. A like plus a fit is a real lead. A like on its own is just a like.

    ### Step 4: Enrich and Prioritize

    Now add whatever contact data you can, email, phone, profile URL, and sort the list by how hard each person engaged, commenters up top. If your tool enriches automatically, great; if not, that's another pass by hand. Either way, work the best names first, because [warm outreach converts 3-5x better than cold](https://expandi.io/blog/state-of-li-outreach-h1-2025/), and your hours are worth more spent on the people most likely to answer.
  </SectionTitle>

<div id="section-5">
  <SectionTitle title="Are Likers or Commenters Better Leads?">
    People ask me this all the time, so: commenters, usually.

    A comment costs something. You read the post, form an opinion, and sign your name to it in front of your whole network. That's a person who's at least a little invested. A like costs nothing, which is exactly why there are so many of them, and why they're worth keeping when you need reach. A post might do five hundred likes and thirty comments; if I have to talk to a hundred people this week, I'm not getting there on commenters alone.

    I tracked this for a quarter because I didn't trust my own gut on it. Commenters replied roughly two to three times more often than likers on the same post, and they closed at 18% versus the likers' 7%. The part that surprised me came later: once I'd worked through the commenters and still had quota to hit, the likers I fell back on still beat anything from a cold purchased list. So likers aren't bad leads. A comment is just a stronger tell, lead for lead, and the numbers kept saying so.
  </SectionTitle>
</div>

<div id="section-6">
  <SectionTitle title="How to Reach Out to LinkedIn Post Engagers Without Being Spammy">
    Finding the right people is maybe half the job. The other half is not torching it the second you message them, and a generic pitch torches it on contact.

    The whole advantage here is that you already know something about this person, so use it. Open with the actual post, "saw your comment on so-and-so's thread about cold outreach," and they know in one line this isn't a blast. If they commented, hand their own words back to them. If they only liked it, talk about the topic instead. (This works just as well for recruiting, by the way, same logic, different ask.) Then lead with something useful before you ask for anything, an article, a number, one decent insight, because going straight for the meeting is where most people lose the thread. And keep connection requests short. You get 300 characters, which is plenty for a real hook and nowhere near enough for your life story.

    None of this is a hunch. [LinkedIn messages reply at 5-20% against 1-10% for cold email](https://belkins.io/blog/linkedin-outreach-study), and just [personalizing the message lifts replies by about 30%](https://www.linkedin.com/) per LinkedIn's own 2024 figures. You did the hard part by finding people who already care. Don't hand it back with a lazy template.
