<div id="what-are-linkedin-voice-messages">
  <SectionTitle title="1) What Are LinkedIn Voice Messages (and Why Most People Get Them Wrong)">
    <p>A LinkedIn voice message is a 60-second audio recording you can send to first-degree connections through the LinkedIn mobile app. Think of it as a voicemail that lives in their inbox.</p>

    <p>The mechanics are about as simple as it gets. Open the LinkedIn app on your phone (and yes, desktop users, this one is mobile-only), go to your messages, tap a connection, and you'll see a microphone icon. Hold it down, record your message, and release to send.</p>

    <p>The catch that most articles skip right past is that <strong>you can only send voice messages to first-degree connections</strong>: people you're already connected with. That rules out prospects you dug up on Sales Navigator, and it rules out anyone you're trying to reach with a cold InMail.</p>

    <p>I'd argue that limitation is a feature, not a bug. It forces you to connect first, which means that <Link href="/blog/connection-requests">connection request</Link> becomes your first touchpoint and the voice message becomes your second. By the time they hear your voice, you're not a total stranger anymore.</p>

    <p>So the interesting part was never how to send a voice message. It's which of your connections actually deserve one.</p>
  </SectionTitle>
</div>

<div id="why-voice-messages-work">
  <SectionTitle title="2) Why Voice Messages Get 40% Response Rates (When Done Right)">
    <p>According to sales trainer <a href="https://reply.io/blog/linkedin-voice-messages/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Morgan Ingram's data</a>, LinkedIn voice messages achieve a <strong>40% response rate</strong> - nearly 4x higher than the <strong>10.3% average</strong> for text-based LinkedIn DMs. Research from <a href="https://lagrowthmachine.com/linkedin-voice-messages/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">La Growth Machine</a> backs this up, showing voice messages boost reply rates by <strong>up to 35%</strong> compared to plain text.</p>

    <p>There are a few reasons the format works as well as it does. The first is novelty. Most salespeople still default to text, so an actual voice is different enough to make someone stop scrolling. In an inbox full of "Hey [First Name], I noticed..." templates, a human voice gets attention simply by not being one more block of copy.</p>

    <p>The second is human connection. Tone, warmth, the little bits of personality, all of it survives in audio in a way it never does in text. It's much harder to ignore a real person talking than to delete another wall of words. I've had prospects tell me they replied only because I "seemed like a real person," which is a low bar, but that's honestly where outreach is right now.</p>

    <p>Third is the effort it signals. You can't copy-paste a voice message, and recording one takes a little work. Prospects can tell, and people tend to reciprocate effort when they notice it.</p>

    <p>The last one is that voice resists templating. Everyone has received the text DM that's obviously a mail-merge with a name dropped in. You can't fake your way through audio the same way, so even when you're saying roughly the same thing to several people, each one still sounds personal.</p>

    <p>What the stats leave out is that the 40% rate isn't a guarantee. It hinges almost entirely on who you're messaging, which is exactly why the gap between <Link href="/blog/warm-vs-cold-outreach-data-breakdown">warm vs cold outreach results</Link> is so wide.</p>
  </SectionTitle>
</div>

<div id="the-missing-piece">
  <SectionTitle title="3) The Missing Piece: Who You Message Matters More Than How">
    <p>When I was running demand gen at my last startup, I made the classic mistake. I read an article about voice messages, got excited, and sent 50 of them to random connections in the same week.</p>

    <p>I got 6 responses. That's a 12% response rate. Not terrible, but a long way from 40%.</p>

    <p>What I'd missed was that <strong>voice messages to random connections still feel cold</strong>. Yes, we were technically "connected" on LinkedIn, but that connection was a generic request from months earlier. They didn't remember me and had no context for why I was suddenly in their ear.</p>

    <p>The thing that actually moves the needle is combining the voice format with warm targeting based on engagement signals. One without the other doesn't do much.</p>

    <p><a href="https://growleads.io/blog/warm-outreach-vs-cold-email/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">B2B research</a> puts warm outreach at 10-34% response rates against just 1-5% for cold outreach, a 5-10x improvement. <a href="https://blog.hubspot.com/sales/sales-statistics" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">HubSpot data</a> goes further: leads contacted within 5 minutes of engagement are 100 times more likely to qualify than those reached after 30 minutes. Timing and context carry an enormous amount of weight here.</p>

    <p>What you're really doing is finding people who have already raised their hand. A like, a comment, or a share on relevant content tells you someone is thinking about the problems you solve this week, not back in some quarter you've forgotten. You can read more about <Link href="/blog/find-warm-leads-social-engagement-signals">finding warm leads from social engagement signals</Link> if you want to go deeper.</p>

    <p>It doesn't much matter how you track that engagement. You can scroll through post notifications and keep a spreadsheet (plenty of people still do exactly this) or you can use a tool like Guffles to extract and filter LinkedIn post engagers automatically. Either way the principle holds: prioritize prospects who have shown recent activity around topics you care about.</p>

    <p>A voice message to someone who commented on an industry post yesterday lands completely differently than the same message to someone you connected with 6 months ago and never spoke to again. That gap is the whole game.</p>

<div id="warm-voice-message-framework">
  <SectionTitle title="4) The Warm Voice Message Framework: 5 Steps to 40% Response Rates">
    <p>After testing this across dozens of campaigns, and making plenty of mistakes along the way, here's the framework that consistently works for me. I call it the 72-Hour Engagement Window, because the timing is what holds the whole thing together.</p>

    <p><strong>Step 1: Find prospects who engaged with relevant content</strong></p>

    <p>Look for comments and likes on industry posts, competitor content, or your own posts. Lean toward comments over likes, since someone who took the time to write something is showing more intent than a passive double-tap. Keep your attention on posts less than 72 hours old. That window is the whole point.</p>

    <p><strong>Step 2: Send a personalized connection request</strong></p>

    <p>Reference their engagement directly. Something like "Hey Sarah, loved your comment on Mike's post about outbound sales dying, would love to connect." Keep it under 300 characters. <a href="https://www.alsona.com/blog/linkedin-connection-request-benchmarks-healthy-acceptance-rate-in-2025" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">LinkedIn data</a> shows personalized connection requests get accepted around 45% of the time versus about 15% for generic ones, which is roughly 3x better. That gap is worth the extra thirty seconds.</p>

    <p><strong>Step 3: Send the voice message within 72 hours of acceptance</strong></p>

    <p>Don't sit on it. Record while the engagement is still fresh. Our data shows that reaching out within 72 hours of engagement lifts response rates by 5-7x compared to waiting a week, because the connection context is still sitting near the top of their mind.</p>

    <p><strong>Step 4: Reference their specific engagement in the message</strong></p>

    <p>Open with something like "I saw your comment on [post topic]..." That single line proves you're not blasting everyone, that you actually paid attention, and it quietly turns cold outreach into a warm conversation.</p>

    <p><strong>Step 5: End with a clear, low-friction CTA</strong></p>

    <p>"Let's schedule a call" is too big an ask for a first voice message. Try "Would you be open to a quick conversation about this?" instead and give them an easy yes or no. If they don't respond, our guide on <Link href="/blog/how-many-follow-up-emails">how many follow-ups to send</Link> covers what to do next.</p>

    <p>The part people underestimate is that sending the voice message is the easy bit. Each one takes about 30 seconds. The real bottleneck is figuring out who to send them to, and manually tracking engagement across dozens of industry posts is genuinely draining. I did it by hand for three weeks and it was miserable.</p>

    <p>That's the gap a tool like Guffles fills. Apollo runs $300+ and ZoomInfo $500+ for cold data, so the savings are real, but the bigger win is that the leads come in warm.</p>

<div id="scripts-that-work">
  <SectionTitle title="5) Voice Message Scripts That Actually Reference Engagement">
    <p>Sales experts recommend keeping LinkedIn voice messages to 20-30 seconds: long enough to introduce yourself and offer something useful, short enough to respect your prospect's time. Here are three scripts I've tested that consistently get responses. Treat them as starting points and rewrite them in your own voice.</p>

    <p><strong>Script 1: After they commented on an industry post</strong></p>

    <p>"Hey [Name], this is [Your name], we just connected and I wanted to send a quick voice note instead of another text. I saw your comment on [Author]'s post about [topic] and really liked your point about [specific thing they said]. I actually work in [related area] and would love to swap notes sometime. No pressure, but if you're ever open to a quick chat, just let me know. Either way, appreciate the connection."</p>

    <p><strong>Script 2: After they liked your content</strong></p>

    <p>"Hey [Name], [Your name] here. Thanks for connecting, and for the like on my post about [topic]. Always good to meet someone else who's thinking about this stuff. I help [description of what you do], so if that's ever relevant to what you're working on, I'd love to hear what you're seeing on your end. Feel free to reply here, or just ignore this if the timing's not right. Talk soon."</p>

    <p><strong>Script 3: After they engaged with a competitor's post</strong></p>

    <p>"Hi [Name], [Your name] here, thanks for accepting. I noticed you commented on [Competitor]'s post about [topic]. I actually have a slightly different take on that, we've been seeing [brief insight]. Would love to share what's working for us if you're curious. No pitch, just perspective. Let me know if you'd be up for a quick chat."</p>

    <p>What ties these together is that each one references the specific engagement, keeps a casual and non-salesy tone, and ends with a soft ask. "If you're open to it" pulls far better than "let's book a call Tuesday at 3pm," and that closing line matters more than most people expect. When you're working on the very first message, our guide on <Link href="/blog/linkedin-first-message">crafting the perfect LinkedIn first message</Link> goes deeper.</p>
  </SectionTitle>
</div>

<div id="best-practices-and-mistakes">
  <SectionTitle title="6) Best Practices and Common Mistakes">
    <p><strong>Best Practices</strong></p>

    <p>Keep the message to 20-30 seconds. That's long enough to add value and short enough to respect their time. LinkedIn lets you run up to 60 seconds, but a full minute is far too long. If you can't make your point in 30, you're probably overcomplicating it.</p>

    <p>Smile while you record, because people really can hear it. It sounds cheesy, I know, but I've A/B tested it and the messages recorded with a smile pull better responses. I have no idea why, and I've stopped trying to explain it.</p>

    <p>Use their name twice: once at the start to grab attention, and once when you make your ask so the request feels personal rather than tacked on.</p>

    <p>Find a quiet spot to record. Coffee shop clatter reads as unprofessional, so look for somewhere without background chatter. I learned that one the embarrassing way.</p>

    <p>One take is plenty. Small stumbles make you sound human, and obsessing over a flawless take tends to push you in the opposite direction, where you come across stiff and robotic.</p>

    <p><strong>Common Mistakes</strong></p>

    <p>Leading with your pitch is the fastest way to get ignored. Earn the right to sell first. A first voice message should build a little connection, not try to close a deal.</p>

    <p>Failing to reference why you connected makes you generic, and generic gets deleted. If you never mention their engagement or anything specific about them, you sound like everyone else in their inbox.</p>

    <p>Asking for too much scares people off. "Let's do a 30-minute call" is a heavy first request, so start much smaller than feels natural.</p>

    <p>Sending to everyone defeats the purpose. Ten voice messages to genuinely warm leads will beat a hundred to random connections, which is exactly why <Link href="/blog/linkedin-engagement-leads">turning LinkedIn engagement into leads</Link> matters so much.</p>

    <p>Waiting too long quietly kills it. The 72-hour window is real, and once it closes the engagement context fades and you're effectively cold again.</p>
  </SectionTitle>
