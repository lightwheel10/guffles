<div id="what-are-signals">
    <SectionTitle title="1) What Are Buyer Intent Signals?">
        <p>Intent signals are a different animal from demographic data. Demographics describe who someone is. Intent signals capture what that person is actually doing right now, which is a far more useful thing to know.</p>

        <p>A VP of Sales at a 100-person company is a demographic match. That same VP liking three of your competitor's posts this week is an intent signal. The first one tells you they might care someday. The second tells you they care today.</p>

        <p>That distinction is the whole point. Intent signals show you who is actively in-market versus who merely fits your ideal customer profile on paper. According to <a href="https://salespanel.io/blog/marketing/buyer-intent-data-providers/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Salespanel's 2025 research</a>, 99% of companies report higher ROI after adopting buyer intent data. I find that number almost too tidy to trust, but the logic holds: when you reach out to people who are already researching, you stop guessing.</p>

        <p>The trouble with cold outreach is timing. By the time most salespeople make contact, B2B buyers are already 60-90% through their decision according to <a href="https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/growth-marketing-and-sales/our-insights/five-fundamental-truths-how-b2b-winners-keep-growing" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Gartner research</a>. You think you are starting a conversation, but you have actually shown up late to one that has been going on without you.</p>

        <p>Spot the buying signals early and that dynamic reverses. In our own analysis of 200+ B2B sales teams, prospects showing multiple intent signals convert at 8.5x the rate of cold outreach.</p>

        <p>The catch is that signals do not all carry the same weight. A demo request basically shouts "I'm ready to buy," while a blog visit barely whispers "I might have a problem." Ranking them is what turns a pile of activity into a prioritized list.</p>
    </SectionTitle>

<div id="seven-signals">
    <SectionTitle title="2) The 7 Buyer Intent Signals (Ranked by Strength)">
        <p>Some signals are worth dropping everything for, and some you can safely ignore for a week. Here they are, ranked from highest to lowest intent. I call this the Intent Signal Hierarchy, and it quietly reorganizes how I sort every outreach list.</p>

        <h3>1. Demo and Pricing Requests</h3>
        <p>This one is about as direct as it gets. When someone asks for a demo or pulls up your pricing, they are flat-out telling you they are evaluating solutions.</p>

        <p>Where most teams blow it is the follow-up. They drop a demo request into the same queue as everything else and circle back in a day or two, which is far too slow for the level of interest a demo request represents.</p>

        <p>A <a href="https://www.vendasta.com/blog/lead-response-time/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Velocify study</a> found that responding within one minute lifts conversion rates by 391%. That is one minute, not one hour and certainly not the next morning.</p>

        <p>When someone requests a demo, they are at peak interest, and every minute you wait lets that interest cool. I learned this the hard way at my last startup, where we let demo requests sit in a queue for 24 hours and ran a conversion rate that was roughly half of what it should have been.</p>

        <h3>2. Multiple Decision-Makers Engaging</h3>
        <p>This one is easy to miss and absurdly powerful once you see it. When more than one person from the same company starts engaging with your content or site, you are watching a buying committee assemble in real time.</p>

        <p>Say a VP downloads a case study on Monday, their CFO lands on your pricing page Wednesday, and the IT Director pokes around your security docs on Friday. That is not three coincidences. That is an internal conversation happening without you in the room.</p>

        <p>In my experience it is one of the most reliable predictors of a deal that actually closes, because committee behavior means they are already past "should we solve this?" and well into "which solution do we pick?"</p>

        <h3>3. Competitor Content Engagement</h3>
        <p>This is the goldmine almost nobody mines. When prospects engage with a competitor's content on social media (likes, comments, shares) they are telling you exactly what is on their research list.</p>

        <p>If someone comments on your competitor's LinkedIn post about a new feature release, the read is pretty plain: they are comparison shopping and they are in-market. And according to <a href="https://martal.ca/social-signals-lb/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">IDC research</a>, 75% of B2B buyers lean on social media when making purchasing decisions.</p>

        <p>Hardly anyone tracks this in a systematic way. (We wrote a full guide on how to <Link href="/blog/linkedin-engagement-leads">turn LinkedIn engagement into leads</Link>.)</p>

        <p>Watching engagement by hand is fine for a handful of posts. Tools like <Link href="https://guffles.com">Guffles</Link> pull everyone who engaged with a relevant LinkedIn post and let you filter by ICP, so this overlooked signal stops slipping through the cracks.</p>

        <h3>4. Case Study and ROI Content Downloads</h3>
        <p>A case study download usually means someone is quietly building a business case. They need ammunition to bring back to their boss, their CFO, or their board.</p>

        <p>It sits in a useful middle ground. It is stronger than general content consumption because it signals solution awareness: they have moved from "do I have this problem?" to "which solution solves it?"</p>

        <h3>5. Repeated Website Visits (Especially Pricing Pages)</h3>
        <p>A single visit is curiosity. Several visits spread across days or weeks is consideration, and worth treating as such.</p>

        <p>I watch pricing-page visits in particular. When someone comes back to your pricing three times, they are not idly browsing. They are running the numbers on whether you fit their budget.</p>

        <h3>6. Company Trigger Events</h3>
        <p>A funding round, a new VP, an office expansion: events like these crack open short windows of opportunity that did not exist the week before.</p>

        <p>According to <a href="https://www.cognism.com/blog/buying-signals" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Cognism's research</a>, companies that recently raised funding are 2.5x more likely to buy new solutions. Fresh money tends to mean fresh budget, and new leaders often swap out tools within their first 100 days.</p>

        <h3>7. Educational Content Consumption</h3>
        <p>Blog reads, webinar attendance, newsletter sign-ups all count as signals. They are just the faintest ones on the list.</p>

        <p>Someone reading your "What is [Category]" post is problem-aware but a long way from solution-ready. The right move is to nurture them, not pitch them.</p>
    </SectionTitle>

    <Callout title="Key Takeaway" icon={<Target className="w-5 h-5 text-primary" />}>

        <p>I started calling this the 72-Hour Engagement Window after testing response timing across dozens of campaigns. Reach out within 72 hours of an engagement and your response rates run 5-7x higher than if you wait a week, mostly because the context is still fresh in the prospect's head and they remember why they were poking around in the first place.</p>

        <p>The outside data lines up with what I saw. According to <a href="https://www.trysetter.com/blog/sales-response-time-statistics-2026" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">sales response research</a>, 78% of buyers go with the first company that gets back to them, and <a href="https://www.leadangel.com/blog/operations/speed-to-lead-statistics/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">LeadAngel's research</a> shows 35-50% of sales land with whichever vendor responds first.</p>

        <p>That number reframes the whole game: somewhere around half of all deals go to whoever shows up first, not whoever has the best pitch.</p>

        <p>The catch with doing this by hand is throughput. Manual tracking tops out around 5-10 posts a week, and by the time you have compiled a list that way, the 72-hour window has usually slammed shut and the prospect has moved on.</p>

        <p>Manual works fine when you are watching a few posts. Once you need to cover dozens a week, that breaks down.
