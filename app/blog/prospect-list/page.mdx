<div id="section-3">
    <SectionTitle title="3) The 4-Step Process to Build Your Prospect List in 30 Minutes">
        This is the exact process I run when I sit down to build a list from nothing.
    </SectionTitle>

    <h3>Step 1: Define Your ICP (5 minutes)</h3>

    <p>Get specific. "Salespeople" is not an ICP. "VP of Sales at B2B SaaS companies with 50-200 employees" is.</p>

    <p>If you already have customers, profile your best ones. What industry are they in, what size are the companies, and which job titles actually sign the checks? Write it down. You cannot find the right people if you have never bothered to describe them.</p>

    <h3>Step 2: Choose Your Data Source (2 minutes)</h3>

    <p>You have three real options:</p>
    <ul>
        <li><strong>Manual</strong>: LinkedIn free search, Google (free, slower)</li>
        <li><strong>Engagement-based</strong>: Find people already interacting with relevant content (faster, warmer)</li>
        <li><strong>Database tools</strong>: Apollo, ZoomInfo (complete, expensive)</li>
    </ul>

    <p>The source you pick is what determines lead quality. A demographic match gets you cold leads, while demonstrated interest gets you warm ones. Same effort, very different outcomes.</p>

    <h3>Step 3: Collect and Verify Contact Information (15 minutes)</h3>

    <p>This is where most people quietly waste the bulk of their time. <a href="https://reply.io/blog/b2b-list-building/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">B2B data decays by 2.1% per month</a> (Reply.io), which works out to 22.5% of your list going stale within a year if you never touch it.</p>

    <p>So verify emails before you add them, and use LinkedIn profiles as your anchor when something does not line up. Keep everything in a spreadsheet or CRM with consistent fields: name, title, company, email, phone, LinkedIn URL, and any engagement notes you picked up along the way.</p>

    <h3>Step 4: Score and Segment Leads by Priority (8 minutes)</h3>

    <p>Not every lead deserves the same attention, so split them into three buckets:</p>

    <ul>
        <li>Hot leads are engaged and an ICP match, so contact them immediately.</li>
        <li>Warm leads are engaged or a strong ICP match, so drop them into a sequence.</li>
        <li>Cool leads are a demographic match only, so nurture them before you pitch.</li>
    </ul>

    <p>Then start with the hot leads, every time. The whole point of scoring is to stop treating a stranger and a hand-raiser as if they are the same opportunity.</p>
</div>

<div id="section-5">
    <SectionTitle title="5) Common Mistakes That Kill Your Response Rates">
        I have made every one of these mistakes, some of them more than once, so consider this the shortcut around my own pain.
    </SectionTitle>

    <p><strong>1. Prioritizing quantity over quality</strong></p>
    <p>500 cold contacts feel productive when you are staring at a fresh spreadsheet, but 50 engaged prospects will generate more pipeline. I have tested this across hundreds of campaigns and the smaller, warmer list wins consistently.</p>

    <p><strong>2. Skipping verification</strong></p>
    <p>Inaccurate contact data wastes <a href="https://taskdrive.com/sales/sales-statistics/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">27.3% of sales reps' time</a>, which adds up to 546 hours per year per rep (TaskDrive, 2025). One hour spent verifying tends to save ten hours later chasing bounced emails.</p>

    <p><strong>3. Ignoring engagement signals</strong></p>
    <p>A demographic match without intent is just a guess wearing a nicer outfit. When someone comments on a competitor's post about the exact problem you solve, that is a signal, and ignoring it is leaving money on the table.</p>

    <p><strong>4. Letting your list go stale</strong></p>
    <p>That 2.1% monthly decay catches up with you fast. A list you built six months ago is already missing a meaningful chunk of its accuracy, so you either clean it or you stop trusting it.</p>

    <p><strong>5. Generic outreach to a targeted list</strong></p>
    <p>You put real effort into building a good list, so do not torch it with "Hi &#123;First_Name&#125;, I'd love to connect." Reference something concrete instead: the post they engaged with, recent company news, anything that proves you actually noticed them.</p>
</div>

<div id="section-6">
    <SectionTitle title="6) Keeping Your Prospect List Fresh">
        A prospect list is not a one-time project. It is a living document that quietly rots if you ignore it.
    </SectionTitle>

    <p>Call it the 22.5% problem. B2B contact data decays by 2.1% every month because people change jobs, companies get acquired, and emails bounce, so within a year nearly a quarter of your list is effectively garbage.</p>

    <p>Here is the routine I stick to:</p>

    <ul>
        <li>Clean the list monthly: remove bounces, update job changes, and flag contacts who have gone quiet.</li>
        <li>Prospect continuously by adding 5-10 new warm leads a week instead of batch-building once a quarter.</li>
        <li>Re-engage on new signals, because someone who went cold months ago might be showing interest again on LinkedIn if you are paying attention.</li>
    </ul>

    <p>The teams that win are not the ones sitting on the biggest lists. They are the ones who keep a smaller list accurate and current, week after week.</p>
</div>
